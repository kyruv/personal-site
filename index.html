About

  Personal

I'm a 3rd-year PhD student in the Department of Computer Science at the University of Colorado Boulder, and a researcher in the Collaborative AI and Robotics (CAIRO) Lab, advised by Brad Hayes.

I graduated undergrad from Cornell University in 2018, where I majored in Computer Science, specializing in and conducting research into artificial intelligence and robotics in the Robot Personal Assistants Lab (RPAL).

Since 2016, I have spent five summers interning at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory in Pasadena, California. I have worked on projects across the Instrument Data System (IDS) and Rover Planning System (RPS) for multiple Martian surface missions - the Curiosity rover, the InSight lander, and the recently-landed Perseverance rover. In the summer of 2019, I had the incredible opportunity to help "drive" Curiosity across multiple plan sequencing cycles, where I accrued a career Martian odometry of 65 meters!

Some of my interests outside of work include exploring the natural world, falling down Wikipedia rabbit holes, observational astronomy, aviation and space travel, and traditional animation.

  Research

Autonomous systems are steadily becoming more capable, especially through the use of data-driven learned models. Despite the substantial advantages modern autonomy techniques present, they possess some key weaknesses that hinder their deployability in safety-critical systems (such as spacecraft, medical devices, road vehicles, etc.) where they could potentially be most beneficial. Namely, learned models produced through these techniques are inherently uninterpretable by humans, which can lead to unforseen, potentially dangerous failures ultimately attributable to mischaracterization of risk.

To help address these concerns, I'm interested in creating risk-aware shared autonomy architectures for robotic task and motion planning, wherein human operator intuition is used to strategically and continuously shape and direct learning-based autonomous controllers. I hypothesize that such hybrid architectures will simulataneously reduce operator workload and add considerable plan flexibility when compared to traditional methods of robot operation, as well as reduce risk and unintended agent behavior when compared to state-of-the-art autonomous approahces.

This research can be broken down into two primary thrusts, forming two sides of a bi-directional shared autonomy architecture; human in the loop planning (methods for human operators to efficiently steer and augment optimization-based planners), and human interpretable risk assessment (improving the situational awareness of operators by algorithmically presenting them with autonomous agents' understanding of sources of plan risk). Underlying both these thrusts is a shared emphasis on effective interface design; allowing operators to inspect model correctness and make informed decisions, while making the most of their cognitive abilities.
